Self Introduction

"Finding My Path: Why I Try Different Tech Jobs"

Professional Journey & Vision

I have a big dream for my career. Some students wait until they graduate to choose a job. But for me, I want to try it right now. I am very interested in UI/UX Design, System Analysis, and Prompt Engineering. I do not do this because I am confused. I do this so I can have clear choices for my future career when I graduate.

In 2025, I got the chance to work on real projects. I worked as both a UI/UX Designer and a System Analyst at Telkom and Dnet. At Telkom, I helped make a public service application. I learned how to make an app that is easy for people to use. At Dnet, I worked on an internal psychological testing website. It was challenging, but I learned how to connect a good design with a system that works well.

Now, I am doing another project. I am helping to build an internal website for PLN. I use my design and system analyst skills to solve their problems. Besides that, I am also learning Prompt Engineering. This means I learn how to give good instructions to AI. Using AI helps me work faster and gives me better ideas for my design and system tasks.

Some people say I should only focus on one job right now. They think trying many things at the same time is a bad idea. I agree that focus is good. But I believe trying different roles in the real world is better for a student. Technology is very big. Trying these jobs helps me understand how everything connects.

In conclusion, my experiences as a UI/UX Designer, System Analyst, and Prompt Engineer are very important to me. They give me direction. When I finally graduate, I will not be confused anymore. I will have strong skills and clear options for my future.

TRANSLATING RIGID GOVERNMENT WORKFLOWS INTO INTUITIVE DIGITAL EXPERIENCES

Dealing with the government is often confusing and takes a lot of time for people because the rules are too strict. Today, many governments are trying to make things easier by building digital apps or "GovTech" for public services. However, just moving paper forms to a screen without fixing the system logic makes the app hard to use, and people still get confused. Because of this, turning rigid government rules into an easy-to-use digital platform needs more than just good visual design. It really needs clear system analysis, good information architecture, and a user-friendly flow. By looking at the process of making a public service super app, this essay will show how these steps can connect strict government rules with easy access for users.

First of all, a successful government app must start with a very clear structure. This structure is known as Information Architecture. When we look at a government requirement document, it usually looks like a thick and confusing law book. The designer's job is to be a translator between the government and normal people. A city can have dozens of different public services. There are features for reporting citizen problems, calling emergency numbers, and asking for business permits. If an app just throws all these features on the home screen randomly, users will feel overwhelmed and probably delete the app right away. To solve this, a system analyst needs to group these services based on what is most important for the users. By organizing them into simple bottom menus like Home, Activity, and Profile, the app becomes much easier to use. This kind of logical mapping proves that building a good app is mostly about system analysis before drawing any design on the screen.

Secondly, the app needs to have a very simple and friendly user flow to beat the strict bureaucracy. Let us take a complex example, like applying for an official permit. In the real world, this usually means people must carry thick folders to many different desks, collect physical stamps, and wait for a very long time. In a digital app, we have to cut out all that confusion. We can design a straight step-by-step journey from start to finish. Users just need to follow simple instructions on their phone. Furthermore, a good design must also think about real-world problems. For example, what happens if a user is filling out a form but suddenly realizes they forgot one important document? A smart app will provide a "Save Draft" button. Because of this small feature, they do not have to start from the beginning again later. Adding a clear status tracker also helps citizens know the progress of their permit. This shows true empathy and makes the rules feel more human.

Finally, after the logic and flow are completely ready, the app needs a clean visual design and a solid system to support it. For a government app, trust and accessibility are everything. Designers should use clean colors, big text, and a lot of empty space so older people can read easily. To make the work faster and consistent, designers build a "Design System." This is like a box of Lego pieces containing standard buttons and forms. This helps developers build hundreds of pages easily. Besides that, since government projects often have tight deadlines, designers can work smart by using AI tools like ChatGPT to make custom 3D illustrations. Instead of drawing from zero, AI can quickly make friendly pictures for empty screens or error pages. By combining a clean design system and smart AI tools, the final output becomes a complete digital ecosystem that is beautiful, fast to build, and very easy for the public to understand.

In conclusion, changing strict government rules into helpful digital apps requires much more than just pretty screens. As we have seen with the public service super app, the real secret is focusing on clear system analysis, organizing the information well, and building friendly user flows. By grouping complex services into simple menus and adding helpful features like a "Save Draft" button, the government can truly help its citizens. Finally, using a clean design system and smart AI tools makes everything faster and easier to build. Ultimately, good digital government services are about showing empathy and making hard bureaucracy feel simple and human.
